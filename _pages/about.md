Hi! I’m Robert, a marine ecologist with a Ph.D. from the University of Maine, where my research with Bob Steneck and Damian Brady integrated field surveys, scientific diving, and quantitative modeling to understand ecological interactions in coastal systems. My work bridges hands‑on marine science, data‑driven analysis, and management‑focused research that supports real‑world decision‑making.

<p align="center">
<img src="/images/DrysuitSurface.JPG" width="65%" />
</p>

My dissertation examined how ecological interactions and environmental change shape American lobster populations in coastal Maine, integrating field surveys, comparative analyses, and quantitative modeling. I began by using diving surveys across multiple habitats to quantify shifts in population densities and evaluate how intraspecific competition may have changed over time. Building on that foundation, I conducted the first integrated comparison of all six benthic lobster surveys in Maine, showing that population trends are consistent across survey types, geographic regions, oceanographic conditions, and life stages. I then developed a length‑based population model to evaluate gear‑specific vulnerability and to explore whether fisheries‑induced selection on trap‑entry behavior could influence observed size structure and survey biases. Together, this work provides decision‑relevant insights for fisheries management in a rapidly changing Gulf of Maine.

While completing my M.S. in Marine Policy at the University of Maine, I interned with the Maine Department of Marine Resources, where I conducted a detailed review of the Atlantic Large Whale Take Reduction Plan’s regulatory history to clarify how gillnet rules have evolved over time. Building on that work, I designed a survey for gillnet fishers in Maine and New England to document gear configurations and fishing practices. The resulting information fed directly into an ALWTRP decision‑support tool and helped managers evaluate how proposed right‑whale protection measures could affect the gillnet fleet.

Another highlight of my graduate work was contributing to long‑term monitoring on Bonaire’s coral reefs. I conducted a week of fish surveys during the 20th year of the island’s monitoring program, working alongside STINAPA, Bonaire’s national park authority. I also helped produce the annual report submitted to STINAPA, which has played an important role in documenting the effectiveness of local conservation measures and the persistence of one of the Caribbean’s few documented resilient reef systems.

Before moving to Maine, I spent a year working after earning my B.S. at Rutgers University. During that time, I served as the Head Sailing Instructor at Monmouth Boat Club and worked at the Haskin Shellfish Research Laboratory under Daphne Monroe on a range of coastal and shellfish ecology projects. While at Rutgers, I balanced my coursework with hands‑on research experience and spent much of my time sailing and racing with the Rutgers University Sailing Team, which shaped both my connection to the water and my interest in applied marine science.
